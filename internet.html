Big Picture

The Internet started as a research project designed to connect computers across long distances. Over time, packet switching, TCP/IP, and the World Wide Web turned it into a public network used for communication, business, entertainment, and online communities.

1960s–1970s: Early Networks

  • Goal: connect computers so researchers can share resources and information
  • 1969: ARPANET begins (one of the first large-scale packet-switched networks)
  • 1971: email becomes widely used and shows that networks connect people—not just machines
  • 1970s: more networks appear, and the need to link different networks becomes clear

1980s–1990s: Standards & The Web

  • 1983: TCP/IP becomes a major standard, allowing different networks to connect reliably
  • 1984: DNS makes the Internet easier to use by introducing domain names
  • Late 1980s: NSFNET expands Internet access across the academic world
  • 1990–1993: the World Wide Web and early browsers make the Internet user-friendly
  • Mid–1990s: commercial ISPs help the Internet grow into a public service

2000s–Today: Broadband, Mobile, and Cloud

  • Broadband: faster connections replace dial-up and support streaming, downloads, and real-time services
  • Mobile Internet: smartphones and Wi-Fi make the Internet accessible almost everywhere
  • Cloud platforms: services run on large data centers, powering social media, video, and online gaming
  • Today: the Internet supports massive global communities, real-time communication, and cross-platform multiplayer

Why this matters for multiplayer gaming

Faster, more stable Internet made real-time multiplayer normal—supporting matchmaking, voice chat, live updates, and global communities.
